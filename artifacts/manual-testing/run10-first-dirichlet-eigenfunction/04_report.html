First Dirichlet eigenfunction via the Rayleigh quotient

First Dirichlet eigenfunction via the Rayleigh quotient. Let \(\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^d\) be a bounded domain with sufficiently regular boundary. Define \[ \lambda_1 := \inf\left\{ \int_\Omega |\nabla u|^2\,dx :u\in H_0^1(\Omega),\ \int_\Omega u^2\,dx = 1 \right\}. \] Then the infimum is attained: there exists \(u_1\in H_0^1(\Omega)\) with \[ \int_\Omega u_1^2\,dx=1 \] such that \[ \lambda_1=\int_\Omega |\nabla u_1|^2\,dx. \] Moreover, \(u_1\) satisfies the weak eigenvalue equation \[ \int_\Omega \nabla u_1\cdot\nabla\varphi\,dx = \lambda_1 \int_\Omega u_1\varphi\,dx \qquad \forall \varphi\in H_0^1(\Omega). \]

Informal statement:

There exists \(u_1\in H_0^1(\Omega)\) with \(\int_\Omega u_1^2\,dx=1\) such that \(\lambda_1=\int_\Omega |\nabla u_1|^2\,dx\), and for every \(\varphi\in H_0^1(\Omega)\), \[ \int_\Omega \nabla u_1\cdot\nabla\varphi\,dx = \lambda_1 \int_\Omega u_1\varphi\,dx. \]

Informal proof:

Take a minimizing sequence \((u_n)\subset H_0^1(\Omega)\) with \(\int_\Omega u_n^2\,dx=1\) and \(\int_\Omega |\nabla u_n|^2\,dx\to \lambda_1\). By compactness and lower semicontinuity, extract \(u_1\in H_0^1(\Omega)\) that is admissible and attains the infimum. Then apply the constrained Euler-Lagrange/Lagrange multiplier argument at the minimizer to obtain \(\int_\Omega \nabla u_1\cdot\nabla\varphi\,dx=\mu\int_\Omega u_1\varphi\,dx\) for all \(\varphi\in H_0^1(\Omega)\). Testing with \(\varphi=u_1\) and using \(\int_\Omega u_1^2\,dx=1\) and the minimizing identity \(\int_\Omega |\nabla u_1|^2\,dx=\lambda_1\), one gets \(\mu=\lambda_1\).

Compactness and lower semicontinuity yield a minimizer

Node id: n1 | Status: candidate_formal

Display label: Attainment

Formalization priority: 1. Rationale: High-yield formal island: direct-method compactness package with weak convergence, compact \(H_0^1\to L^2\) embedding, preservation of the \(L^2\) constraint, and weak lower semicontinuity. This is the main existence burden.

Informal statement:

From any minimizing sequence \((u_n)\subset H_0^1(\Omega)\) with \(\int_\Omega u_n^2\,dx=1\) and \(\int_\Omega |\nabla u_n|^2\,dx\to\lambda_1\), one can extract \(u_1\in H_0^1(\Omega)\) such that \(u_n\rightharpoonup u_1\) weakly in \(H_0^1(\Omega)\), \(u_n\to u_1\) strongly in \(L^2(\Omega)\), \(\int_\Omega u_1^2\,dx=1\), and \[ \int_\Omega |\nabla u_1|^2\,dx=\lambda_1. \]

Informal proof:

The minimizing sequence is bounded in \(H_0^1(\Omega)\). By weak compactness, pass to a subsequence with \(u_n\rightharpoonup u_1\) in \(H_0^1(\Omega)\). By the compact embedding \(H_0^1(\Omega)\hookrightarrow L^2(\Omega)\), after a further subsequence \(u_n\to u_1\) strongly in \(L^2(\Omega)\). Hence \(\int_\Omega u_1^2\,dx=\lim_n\int_\Omega u_n^2\,dx=1\), so \(u_1\) is admissible. Weak lower semicontinuity gives \(\int_\Omega |\nabla u_1|^2\,dx\le \liminf_n\int_\Omega |\nabla u_n|^2\,dx=\lambda_1\). Since \(\lambda_1\) is the infimum over admissible functions and \(u_1\) is admissible, also \(\lambda_1\le \int_\Omega |\nabla u_1|^2\,dx\). Therefore equality holds.

Lagrange multiplier identifies the weak eigenvalue equation

Node id: n2 | Status: informal

Display label: Euler-Lagrange

Informal statement:

If \(u_1\in H_0^1(\Omega)\) is an admissible minimizer of \(u\mapsto \int_\Omega |\nabla u|^2\,dx\) under the constraint \(\int_\Omega u^2\,dx=1\), then there exists \(\mu\in\mathbb{R}\) such that \[ \int_\Omega \nabla u_1\cdot\nabla\varphi\,dx = \mu \int_\Omega u_1\varphi\,dx \qquad \forall \varphi\in H_0^1(\Omega). \] Moreover, if \(\int_\Omega |\nabla u_1|^2\,dx=\lambda_1\), then \(\mu=\lambda_1\).

Informal proof:

Apply the usual constrained Euler-Lagrange argument for the functional \(E(u)=\int_\Omega |\nabla u|^2\,dx\) on the unit \(L^2\)-sphere in \(H_0^1(\Omega)\). This yields a multiplier \(\mu\) with \(\int_\Omega \nabla u_1\cdot\nabla\varphi\,dx=\mu\int_\Omega u_1\varphi\,dx\) for all test functions \(\varphi\in H_0^1(\Omega)\). Taking \(\varphi=u_1\) gives \(\int_\Omega |\nabla u_1|^2\,dx=\mu\int_\Omega u_1^2\,dx\). Using \(\int_\Omega u_1^2\,dx=1\), one gets \(\mu=\int_\Omega |\nabla u_1|^2\,dx\). If \(u_1\) attains the Rayleigh quotient minimum, this equals \(\lambda_1\).

Certified local core for Lagrange multiplier identifies the weak eigenvalue equation

Informal statement:

Assume \(u_1\) satisfies the weak identity \[ a\varphi, \int_\Omega \operatorname{grad\_pair}(u_1,\varphi)= \mu \, \int_\Omega u_1\varphi, \] for every test function \(\varphi\), and assume also that \[ \int_\Omega u_1^2=1, \qquad \int_\Omega \operatorname{grad\_pair}(u_1,u_1)=\lambda_1. \] Then \(\mu=\lambda_1\). This is a narrower conclusion than the full Euler-Lagrange step: it only identifies the multiplier once the weak equation, normalization, and energy value are already known.

Informal proof:

This node records a verified supporting sublemma extracted from the formalization of parent node 'n2'. Test the weak identity with \(\varphi=u_1\). Then \[ \int_\Omega \operatorname{grad\_pair}(u_1,u_1) = \mu \int_\Omega u_1^2. \] By the normalization hypothesis, \(\int_\Omega u_1^2=1\), so the right-hand side is just \(\mu\). By the energy hypothesis, the left-hand side equals \(\lambda_1\). Therefore \(\mu=\lambda_1\).
