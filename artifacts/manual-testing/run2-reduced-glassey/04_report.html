Reduced Glassey blow-up argument

Finite-time blow-up from a virial identity for focusing mass-critical NLS. Let \(u(t,x)\) be a sufficiently regular solution on \(\mathbb{R}^d\) with finite variance. Assume: 1. mass is conserved: \[ \|u(t)\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}=\|u_0\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}=:M>0, \] 2. energy is conserved and negative: \[ E(u(t))=E(u_0)<0, \] 3. the variance \[ V(t)=\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|x|^2|u(t,x)|^2\,dx \] satisfies \[ V''(t)=16E(u_0)<0, \] 4. for every sufficiently regular \(f\), \[ \|f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}^2 \le \frac{2}{d}\,\|x f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\,\|\nabla f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}. \] Then there exists \(T<\infty\) such that \[ \|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\to\infty \quad\text{as } t\uparrow T. \]

Finite-time blow-up from the virial identity

Node id: n0 | Status: informal

Display label: Blow-up conclusion

Informal statement:

There exists \(T<\infty\) such that \(\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\to\infty\) as \(t\uparrow T\).

Informal proof:

Use the lower bound \(M^2\le \frac{2}{d}\sqrt{V(t)}\,\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2}\), obtained from the weighted inequality applied to \(u(t)\), mass conservation \(\|u(t)\|_{L^2}=M\), and \(\|xu(t)\|_{L^2}^2=V(t)\). By the finite-time variance-collapse claim, there is \(T<\infty\) with \(V(t)\to 0\) as \(t\uparrow T\). Since \(M>0\) is fixed, the inequality can remain true only if \(\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\to\infty\) as \(t\uparrow T\).

Weighted inequality gives a gradient lower bound in terms of variance

Node id: n1 | Status: informal

Display label: Mass-variance-gradient inequality

Informal statement:

For every time in the interval of existence, \[ M^2=\|u(t)\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}^2 \le \frac{2}{d}\sqrt{V(t)}\,\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}. \]

Informal proof:

Apply the assumed inequality \[ \|f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}^2\le \frac{2}{d}\,\|xf\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\,\|\nabla f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)} \] with \(f=u(t)\). Mass conservation gives \(\|u(t)\|_{L^2}=M\), so \[ M^2\le \frac{2}{d}\,\|xu(t)\|_{L^2}\,\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2}. \] By definition of the variance, \[ \|xu(t)\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}^2=V(t), \] hence \(\|xu(t)\|_{L^2}=\sqrt{V(t)}\), yielding the claim.

Negative constant virial acceleration forces finite-time collapse of the variance

Node id: n2 | Status: informal

Display label: Finite-time variance collapse

Informal statement:

There exists a finite time \(T>0\) such that \(V(T)=0\); equivalently, \(V(t)\to 0\) as \(t\uparrow T\).

Informal proof:

Since \(E(u_0)<0\), the virial identity gives \[ V''(t)=16E(u_0)<0. \] Thus \(V\) is a quadratic function with negative leading coefficient: integrating twice, \[ V(t)=8E(u_0)t^2+at+b \] for some constants \(a,b\), and the coefficient of \(t^2\) is negative. Therefore \(V(t)\to -\infty\) as \(t\to +\infty\). On the other hand, by definition, \[ V(t)=\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|x|^2|u(t,x)|^2\,dx\ge 0 \] for every time where the solution exists. Hence \(V\) cannot stay positive for all \(t\ge 0\). By continuity, there is a finite \(T>0\) with \(V(T)=0\), and therefore \(V(t)\to 0\) as \(t\uparrow T\).
