Full Glassey blow-up proof

Glassey blow-up argument for the focusing mass-critical nonlinear Schrodinger equation. Consider a sufficiently regular solution \(u(t,x)\) to the focusing mass-critical NLS on \(\mathbb{R}^d\): \[ i\partial_t u+\Delta u=-|u|^{4/d}u. \] Assume: - \(u_0\in H^1(\mathbb{R}^d)\), - \(|x|u_0\in L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)\), - the energy \(E(u_0)\) is negative, where \[ E(u)=\frac12\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|\nabla u|^2\,dx -\frac{1}{2+4/d}\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|u|^{2+4/d}\,dx. \] Then the maximal forward existence time \(T\) is finite and \[ \|u(t)\|_{H^1(\mathbb{R}^d)}\to\infty \quad\text{as }t\uparrow T. \]

Glassey blow-up for focusing mass-critical NLS

Node id: thm_blowup | Status: informal

Display label: Main theorem

Informal statement:

Under the assumptions \(u_0\in H^1\), \(|x|u_0\in L^2\), and \(E(u_0)<0\), the forward maximal lifespan \(T\) of the solution to \(i\partial_tu+\Delta u=-|u|^{4/d}u\) is finite and \(\|u(t)\|_{H^1(\mathbb{R}^d)}\to\infty\) as \(t\uparrow T\).

Informal proof:

Define the variance \(V(t)=\int |x|^2|u(t,x)|^2\,dx\). The virial computation gives \(V''(t)=16E(u(t))=16E(u_0)<0\), so the variance is strictly concave with negative constant second derivative. Since \(V(t)\ge 0\), this forces a finite time \(T\) at which \(V(t)\to 0\) as \(t\uparrow T\). A weighted inequality together with mass conservation gives \(\|u_0\|_{L^2}^2\le \frac{2}{d}\sqrt{V(t)}\,\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2}\). Because the mass is fixed and \(\sqrt{V(t)}\to 0\), one gets \(\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2}\to\infty\), hence also \(\|u(t)\|_{H^1}\to\infty\). Therefore the solution cannot persist globally forward in \(H^1\), so the maximal forward existence time is finite.

Virial second-derivative identity

Node id: virial_identity | Status: informal

Display label: Virial identity

Informal statement:

For \(V(t)=\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|x|^2|u(t,x)|^2\,dx\), one has \[ V''(t)=16E(u(t))=16E(u_0)<0. \]

Informal proof:

Differentiate \(V(t)\) once and use \(u_t=i\Delta u+i|u|^{4/d}u\). The nonlinear contribution in the first derivative is real before multiplication by \(i\), so its real part vanishes, and integration by parts yields \[ V'(t)=4\operatorname{Im}\int x\cdot \nabla u\,\overline u\,dx. \] Differentiate again and substitute \(u_t=i\Delta u+i|u|^{4/d}u\) and \(\overline{u_t}=-i\Delta\overline u-i|u|^{4/d}\overline u\). Splitting linear and nonlinear terms, a standard integration-by-parts computation gives for the linear part \[ 4\operatorname{Re}\int x\cdot \nabla\Delta u\,\overline u\,dx-4\operatorname{Re}\int x\cdot \nabla u\,\Delta\overline u\,dx=8\int |\nabla u|^2\,dx. \] For the nonlinear part, rewrite the real-part contribution as a divergence term and integrate by parts using \(\operatorname{div}(x)=d\), obtaining \[ -\frac{16}{2+4/d}\int |u|^{2+4/d}\,dx. \] Hence \[ V''(t)=8\int |\nabla u|^2\,dx-\frac{16}{2+4/d}\int |u|^{2+4/d}\,dx=16E(u(t)). \] By conservation of energy, \(E(u(t))=E(u_0)\), so \(V''(t)=16E(u_0)<0\).

Weighted \(L^2\) inequality

Node id: weighted_inequality | Status: informal

Display label: Weighted inequality

Informal statement:

For sufficiently regular \(f\), \[ \|f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}^2\le \frac{2}{d}\,\|x f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\,\|\nabla f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}. \]

Informal proof:

Use \(\operatorname{div}(x)=d\) and integrate by parts: \[ \|f\|_{L^2}^2=\int |f|^2\,dx=\frac1d\int (\operatorname{div}x)|f|^2\,dx=-\frac1d\int x\cdot \nabla(|f|^2)\,dx=-\frac{2}{d}\operatorname{Re}\int x\cdot \nabla f\,\overline f\,dx. \] Then apply \(|\operatorname{Re} z|\le |z|\) and Cauchy-Schwarz to obtain \[ \|f\|_{L^2}^2\le \frac{2}{d}\,\|xf\|_{L^2}\,\|\nabla f\|_{L^2}. \]

Negative virial forces finite-time collapse of the variance

Node id: variance_collapse | Status: informal

Display label: Variance collapse

Informal statement:

Since \(V''(t)=16E(u_0)<0\) and \(V(t)\ge 0\), there exists a finite positive time \(T\) such that \(V(T)=0\), equivalently \(V(t)\to 0\) as \(t\uparrow T\).

Informal proof:

The identity \(V''(t)=16E(u_0)<0\) shows that, on its interval of existence, \(V\) is a concave quadratic with negative leading coefficient. Such a function cannot stay nonnegative for all sufficiently large positive times. But by definition \(V(t)=\int |x|^2|u(t,x)|^2\,dx\ge 0\). Therefore the forward evolution cannot continue with positive variance forever; by continuity there is a finite positive time \(T\) at which the nonnegative variance reaches \(0\), and hence \(V(t)\to 0\) as \(t\uparrow T\).

Variance collapse and conserved mass force gradient blow-up

Node id: gradient_blowup | Status: formal_failed

Display label: Gradient blow-up

Formalization priority: 1. Rationale: Best first local target: it is inferentially important for the main theorem, technically self-contained once mass conservation and variance collapse are available, and it packages the key coercive estimate into the actual blow-up conclusion. Formalizing this node discharges more real burden than the standalone weighted inequality, while avoiding the substantially heavier PDE/virial computation in the virial identity.

Informal statement:

Let \(M=\|u_0\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\). Then \[ M^2\le \frac{2}{d}\sqrt{V(t)}\,\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}. \] If \(V(t)\to 0\) as \(t\uparrow T\), it follows that \(\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2}\to\infty\), and therefore \(\|u(t)\|_{H^1}\to\infty\).

Informal proof:

Apply the weighted inequality to \(f=u(t)\). By conservation of mass, \(\|u(t)\|_{L^2}=\|u_0\|_{L^2}=:M\), so \[ M^2\le \frac{2}{d}\,\|xu(t)\|_{L^2}\,\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2}=\frac{2}{d}\sqrt{V(t)}\,\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2}. \] As \(t\uparrow T\), the quantity \(M>0\) stays fixed while \(\sqrt{V(t)}\to 0\). Thus the product estimate can hold only if \(\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2}\to\infty\). Since \[ \|u(t)\|_{H^1}^2=\|u(t)\|_{L^2}^2+\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2}^2 \] and the \(L^2\) norm is conserved, this also implies \(\|u(t)\|_{H^1}\to\infty\).
