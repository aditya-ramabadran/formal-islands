Full Glassey blow-up proof

Glassey blow-up argument for the focusing mass-critical nonlinear Schrodinger equation. Consider a sufficiently regular solution \(u(t,x)\) to the focusing mass-critical NLS on \(\mathbb{R}^d\): \[ i\partial_t u+\Delta u=-|u|^{4/d}u. \] Assume: - \(u_0\in H^1(\mathbb{R}^d)\), - \(|x|u_0\in L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)\), - the energy \(E(u_0)\) is negative, where \[ E(u)=\frac12\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|\nabla u|^2\,dx -\frac{1}{2+4/d}\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|u|^{2+4/d}\,dx. \] Then the maximal forward existence time \(T\) is finite and \[ \|u(t)\|_{H^1(\mathbb{R}^d)}\to\infty \quad\text{as }t\uparrow T. \]

Finite-time blow-up from negative energy

Node id: thm_glassey_blowup | Status: informal

Display label: Glassey blow-up

Informal statement:

Under the stated assumptions, the maximal forward existence time \(T\) is finite and \(\|u(t)\|_{H^1(\mathbb{R}^d)}\to\infty\) as \(t\uparrow T\).

Informal proof:

Let \(V(t)=\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|x|^2|u(t,x)|^2\,dx\). By the virial computation, \(V''(t)=16E(u_0)<0\), so \(V\) is a concave quadratic with negative leading coefficient. Since \(V(t)\ge 0\) by definition, it cannot stay nonnegative for all large positive \(t\); hence there is a finite positive time \(T\) with \(V(T)=0\), and therefore \(V(t)\to 0\) as \(t\uparrow T\). By the weighted inequality applied to \(f=u(t)\) and conservation of mass, if \(M=\|u_0\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\), then \(M^2\le \frac{2}{d}\sqrt{V(t)}\,\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\). Because \(M>0\) is fixed and \(\sqrt{V(t)}\to 0\), it follows that \(\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\to\infty\) as \(t\uparrow T\). Since \(\|u(t)\|_{H^1}^2=\|u(t)\|_{L^2}^2+\|\nabla u(t)\|_{L^2}^2\) and the \(L^2\) norm is conserved, we obtain \(\|u(t)\|_{H^1(\mathbb{R}^d)}\to\infty\). Thus the solution blows up in finite positive time.

Virial identity and negative variance curvature

Node id: lem_virial_negative_second_derivative | Status: informal

Display label: Virial identity

Informal statement:

For \(V(t)=\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|x|^2|u(t,x)|^2\,dx\), one has \[ V'(t)=4\operatorname{Im}\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}x\cdot \nabla u(t,x)\,\overline{u(t,x)}\,dx \] and \[ V''(t)=16E(u(t))=16E(u_0)<0. \]

Informal proof:

Differentiate \(V(t)\) once: \[ V'(t)=2\operatorname{Re}\int |x|^2u_t\overline u\,dx. \] Using \(iu_t+\Delta u=-|u|^{4/d}u\), rewrite \(u_t=i\Delta u+i|u|^{4/d}u\). Substituting into \(V'(t)\), the nonlinear term vanishes because \(\int |x|^2|u|^{2+4/d}\,dx\) is real, so its product with \(i\) is purely imaginary. Integrating by parts in the Laplacian term gives \[ \int |x|^2\Delta u\,\overline u\,dx =-2\int x\cdot \nabla u\,\overline u\,dx-\int |x|^2|\nabla u|^2\,dx, \] and the last term is real, hence disappears after multiplying by \(i\) and taking real parts. Therefore \[ V'(t)=4\operatorname{Im}\int x\cdot \nabla u\,\overline u\,dx. \] Differentiating again and substituting \(u_t=i\Delta u+i|u|^{4/d}u\), \(\overline{u_t}=-i\Delta\overline u-i|u|^{4/d}\overline u\), one splits \(V''(t)\) into linear and nonlinear contributions. A standard integration-by-parts computation yields for the linear part \[ 4\operatorname{Re}\int x\cdot \nabla\Delta u\,\overline u\,dx-4\operatorname{Re}\int x\cdot \nabla u\,\Delta\overline u\,dx=8\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|\nabla u|^2\,dx. \] For the nonlinear part, after rewriting the real-part contribution as a divergence term and integrating by parts using \(\operatorname{div}(x)=d\), one gets \[ -\frac{16}{2+4/d}\int_{\mathbb{R}^d}|u|^{2+4/d}\,dx. \] Hence \[ V''(t)=8\int |\nabla u|^2\,dx-\frac{16}{2+4/d}\int |u|^{2+4/d}\,dx=16E(u(t)). \] By conservation of energy, \(E(u(t))=E(u_0)\), so \(V''(t)=16E(u_0)<0\).

Weighted inequality relating mass, variance, and gradient

Node id: lem_weighted_inequality | Status: formal_failed

Display label: Weighted inequality

Formalization priority: 1. Rationale: Most conservative local island: a short, self-contained inequality proved by integration by parts, the identity div x = d, and Cauchy-Schwarz. It avoids the heavy PDE and time-differentiation machinery required for the virial lemma and is a useful standalone component for the final blow-up argument.

Informal statement:

For sufficiently regular \(f\), \[ \|f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}^2 \le \frac{2}{d}\,\|x f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\,\|\nabla f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}. \]

Informal proof:

Using \(\operatorname{div}(x)=d\), integrate by parts: \[ \|f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}^2 =\int |f|^2\,dx =\frac{1}{d}\int (\operatorname{div}x)|f|^2\,dx =-\frac{1}{d}\int x\cdot \nabla(|f|^2)\,dx =-\frac{2}{d}\operatorname{Re}\int x\cdot \nabla f\,\overline f\,dx. \] Then \(|\operatorname{Re} z|\le |z|\) and Cauchy-Schwarz imply \[ \|f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}^2 \le \frac{2}{d}\,\|x f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}\,\|\nabla f\|_{L^2(\mathbb{R}^d)}. \]
