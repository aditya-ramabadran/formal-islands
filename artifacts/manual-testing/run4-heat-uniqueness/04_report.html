Heat equation uniqueness by the energy method

Uniqueness for the heat equation by the energy method. Let \(u,v\) be sufficiently regular solutions of the heat equation on the interval \((0,L)\) for times \(t \in [0,T]\): \[ u_t = u_{xx}, \qquad v_t = v_{xx}, \] with the same initial data \[ u(x,0)=v(x,0), \] and the same homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions \[ u(0,t)=u(L,t)=v(0,t)=v(L,t)=0. \] Then \(u=v\) on \([0,L]\times[0,T]\).

Uniqueness for the heat equation by the energy method

Node id: n0 | Status: informal

Display label: theorem

Informal statement:

Show that two sufficiently regular solutions of the heat equation with the same initial data and homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions must coincide on \([0,L]\times[0,T]\).

Informal proof:

Reduce to the difference \(w=u-v\), derive an energy identity for \(E(t)=\frac12\int_0^L w(x,t)^2\,dx\), and use that \(E\) is nonincreasing with \(E(0)=0\) to conclude \(E(t)=0\) for all \(t\), hence \(w\equiv 0\) and therefore \(u=v\).

Energy identity and dissipation

Node id: n2 | Status: formal_verified

Display label: energy identity

Formalization priority: 1. Rationale: This is the main formal island: the differentiated energy identity plus integration by parts is the key technical step and the one most worth checking carefully.

Informal statement:

For \(E(t)=\frac12\int_0^L w(x,t)^2\,dx\), one has \(E'(t)=-\int_0^L |w_x(x,t)|^2\,dx\le 0\).

Informal proof:

Differentiate under the integral sign to get \(E'(t)=\int_0^L w\,w_t\,dx\). Using \(w_t=w_{xx}\), integrate by parts in \(x\): \[ \int_0^L w\,w_{xx}\,dx = \bigl[w\,w_x\bigr]_0^L - \int_0^L |w_x|^2\,dx. \] The boundary term vanishes because \(w(0,t)=w(L,t)=0\), leaving \(E'(t)=-\int_0^L |w_x|^2\,dx\le 0\).

Zero initial energy forces \(w\equiv 0\)

Node id: n3 | Status: candidate_formal

Display label: zero energy

Formalization priority: 2. Rationale: This is the decisive monotonicity argument turning the energy identity into uniqueness; it is compact but inferentially essential.

Informal statement:

Since \(E\ge 0\), \(E\) is nonincreasing, and \(E(0)=0\), it follows that \(E(t)=0\) for all \(t\), hence \(w(x,t)=0\) for all \((x,t)\).

Informal proof:

From \(w(x,0)=0\), we have \(E(0)=\frac12\int_0^L 0^2\,dx=0\). Because \(E'(t)\le 0\), the function \(E\) is nonincreasing, and since it is also nonnegative by definition, it cannot drop below \(0\) or rise above its initial value. Therefore \(E(t)=0\) for every \(t\in[0,T]\). A zero \(L^2\)-norm implies \(w(\cdot,t)=0\) for each \(t\), so \(w\equiv 0\).
